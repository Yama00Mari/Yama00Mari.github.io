Study 3: Mass Study

To study the effect of mass on NoC determination, the DNA samples in the PROVEDit database were distributed into 6 distinct categories. These categories were made based on specific mass thresholds of the samples. The mass threshold used were 25, 50, 75, 100, 125, and 150 pg. The samples were divided into 5 sub-groups under each threshold. These sub-groups were created based on two conditions: 1) whether the masses of the contributors were higher or lower than the thresholds and 2) the relative ratios of the contributors of the samples. The sub-categories were “more-equal” (the ratios of the contributors are equal and their masses are higher than the threshold), “more-diff” (the ratios of the contributors are unequal and their masses are higher than the threshold), “middle” (at least 1 contributor’s mass his higher than the threshold and at least 1 contributor’s mass is lower than the threshold), “less-equal” (less than threshold and equal ratios), and “less-diff” (less than threshold and different ratios).

Each of these 5 sub-groups was later divided into 2 groups: training data set (about 80% of the samples) and testing data set (the remaining 20%). Care was taken to attain similar distributions (in terms of the number of contributors) in the training and testing datasets. This is generally referred to as stratified sampling. The training dataset of each of the sub-groups was combined to form the “all” data set, while their testing datasets were combined to form the “master testing set”. Using the Random Forest algorithm with a 10-fold cross-validation, the “master testing set” was tested against 1) the “all” dataset, 2) each sub-group’s training dataset. The results generated from these two tests were compared. The result obtained is presented in Figures 9a-9f.

In Figure 9a, the “Threshold = 25” represents results generated from the 25 pg threshold samples. The “all” column (highlighted in orange) shows the number of errors made by the “all” training data when it was tested against the testing data from the different sub-groups. It can be observed that the “all” sample had a varying number of errors for the different sub-groups. The highest error was obtained from more-diff test data (a total of 21 errors) while the lowest errors were obtained from the less equal sub-group (a total of 5 errors). The total number of errors made by the “all” sample was 39 errors (green box in the “all” column). The diagonal boxes (highlighted in orange) represent errors generated when the training and testing samples were of the same sub-group. The sum of all the errors was found to be 33 (green box in the less-diff column). Generally observing Figures 9a-9f, it can be observed that for thresholds 100 pg and less, the errors made by the “all” file are greater than the combined errors of the sub-groups (when they were tested against their own testing dataset). The 125 and 150 pg thresholds on the other hand showed that the “all” file made fewer errors. Overall, this indicates that dividing the samples into sub-groups was beneficial for samples 100 pg and less but not beneficial for samples 125 and 150 pg. This is mainly due to the fact that at this threshold, the samples are very varied.
